THE POWER RULE

\(f(x) = 2x^3\)

Find the derivative of a variable term like \(2x^3\) by applying the Power Rule:

  Multiply the exponent 3 by the coefficient 2: \((3)2x^3\).

  Subtract 1 from the exponent: \((3)2x^2\).

  Simplify: \(6x^2\).

\(f'(x) = 6x^2\)

NEGATIVE COEFFICIENTS

\(f(x) = -2x^3\)

Negative coefficients require us to multiply by negative numbers when applying the Power Rule.

  Multiply the exponent 3 by the coefficient -2: \((3)-2x^3\).

  Subtract 1 from the exponent: \((3)-2x^2\).

  Simplify: \(-6x^2\).

\(f'(x) = -6x^2\)

FRACTIONAL COEFFICIENTS

\(f(x) = \frac{1}{2}x^3\)

Fractional coefficients require us to multiply by fractions when applying the Power Rule.

  Multiply the exponent 3 by the coefficient \(\frac{1}{2}\): \((3)\frac{1}{2}x^3\).

  Subtract 1 from the exponent: \((3)\frac{1}{2}x^2\).

  Simplify: \(\frac{3}{2}x^2\).

\(f'(x) = \frac{3}{2}x^2\)

BASIC MIX

a. \(f(x) = 2x^4\)    b. \(f(x) = -5x^2\)    c. \(f(x) = \frac{2}{3}x^2\)

This quiz contains a mix of terms with positive whole-number coefficients, negative coefficients, and fractional coefficients.

  Simply apply the Power Rule:

a. \(f(x) = 2x^4\)

  Multiply the exponent 4 by the coefficient 2: \((4)2x^4\).

  Subtract 1 from the exponent: \((4)2x^3\).

  Simplify: \(8x^3\).

\(f'(x) = 8x^3\)


b. \(f(x) = -5x^2\)

  Multiply the exponent 2 by the coefficient -5: \((2)-5x^2\).

  Subtract 1 from the exponent: \((2)-5x\).

  Simplify: \(-10x\).

\(f'(x) = -10x\)


c. \(f(x) = \frac{2}{3}x^2\)

  Multiply the exponent 2 by the coefficient \(\frac{2}{3}\): \((2)\frac{2}{3}x^2\).

  Subtract 1 from the exponent: \((2)\frac{2}{3}x\).

  Simplify: \(\frac{4}{3}x\).

\(f'(x) = \frac{4}{3}x\)

NEGATIVE EXPONENTS

\(f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2}\)

\(\frac{1}{x^2}\) can be rewritten as \(x^{-2}\).

\(x^2\) means multiply by \(x\) two times.

\(x^{-2}\) means divide by \(x\) two times.  \(x^{-2} = \frac{1}{x^2}\)

Essentially, negative exponents "push" the \(x\) term to the denominator.

\(x^{-3} = \frac{1}{x^3}\)

\(2x^{-4} = \frac{2}{x^4}\)

\(\frac{3}{5}x^{-2} = \frac{3}{5x^2}\)

We can easily use the Power Rule to find the derivative of terms with negative exponents.

We can simply rewrite the term using a negative exponent and then take the derivative in the usual way.

\(f(x) = \frac{1}{x^2}\)

    Rewrite as a negative exponent:   \(f(x) = x^{-2}\)

    Multiply the exponent -2 by the coefficient 1:   \((-2)1x^{-2}\).

    Subtract 1 from the exponent:   \((-2)1x^{-3}\).

   Subtract 1 from -2 and it gets more negative and becomes -3

    Simplify:   \(-2x^{-3}\).

   Put it back in its original form (with a positive exponent):  \(-\frac{2}{x^3}\)

\(f'(x) = -\frac{2}{x^3}\)

NEGATIVE COEFFICIENTS

\(f(x) = -\frac{2}{x^3}\)

Negative exponents with negative coefficients require us to multiply a negative by a negative when applying the Power Rule.

    Rewrite as a negative exponent:   \(f(x) = -2x^{-3}\)

    Multiply the exponent -3 by the coefficient -2:   \((-3)(-2)x^{-3}\).

    Subtract 1 from the exponent:   \((-3)(-2)x^{-4}\).

    Simplify:   \(6x^{-4}\).

   Put it back in its original form (with a positive exponent):  \(\frac{6}{x^4}\)

\(f'(x) = \frac{6}{x^4}\)

NEGATIVE EXPONENTS, FRACTIONAL COEFFICIENTS

\(f(x) = \frac{3}{4x^2}\)

Fractional coefficients require us to multiply by fractions when applying the Power Rule.

    Rewrite as a negative exponent:   \(f(x) = \frac{3}{4}x^{-2}\)

    Multiply the exponent -2 by the coefficient \(\frac{3}{4}\):   \((-2)\frac{3}{4}x^{-2}\).

    Subtract 1 from the exponent:   \((-2)\frac{3}{4}x^{-3}\).

    Simplify:   \(-\frac{6}{4}x^{-3}\).

    Reduce the fraction:   \(-\frac{3}{2}x^{-3}\).

   Put it back in its original form (with a positive exponent):  \(-\frac{3}{2x^3}\)

\(f'(x) = -\frac{3}{2x^3}\)

FRACTIONAL EXPONENTS

\(f(x) = x^{\frac{2}{3}}\)

\(\sqrt[3]{x^2}\) can be read as "the cubed root of x-squared".

This can be written as a fractional exponent like this: \(x^{\frac{2}{3}}\)

In a fractional exponent, the numerator is the exponent and the denominator is the root.

We can easily use the Power Rule to find the derivative of terms with fractional exponents.

We can simply rewrite the term using a fractional exponent and then take the derivative in the usual way.

\(f(x) = \sqrt[3]{x^2}\)

    Rewrite as a fractional exponent:   \(f(x) = x^{\frac{2}{3}}\)

    Multiply the exponent \(\frac{2}{3}\) by the coefficient 1:   \((\frac{2}{3})(1)x^{\frac{2}{3}}\)

    Subtract 1 from the exponent:   \((\frac{2}{3})(1)x^{-\frac{1}{3}}\).

   Subtract 1 \(\left(or \frac{3}{3}\right)\) from \(\frac{2}{3}\) and you get \(-\frac{1}{3}\).

    Simplify:   \(\frac{2}{3}x^{-\frac{1}{3}}\).

    Rewrite it with a positive exponent:  \(\frac{2}{3x^{\frac{1}{3}}}\)

   Put it back in its original form (with a radical):  \(\frac{2}{3\sqrt[3]{x}}\)

\(f'(x) = \frac{2}{3\sqrt[3]{x}}\)

FRACTIONAL EXPONENTS AND NEGATIVE COEFFICIENTS

\(f(x) = -3\sqrt{x^3}\)

Fractional exponents with negative coefficients require us to multiply fractions by negative numbers.

    Rewrite as a fractional exponent:   \(f(x) = -3x^{\frac{3}{2}}\)

    Multiply the exponent \(\frac{3}{2}\) by the coefficient -3:   \((\frac{3}{2})(-3)x^{\frac{3}{2}}\).

    Subtract 1 from the exponent:   \((\frac{3}{2})(-3)x^{\frac{1}{2}}\).

    Simplify:   \(-\frac{9}{2}x^{\frac{1}{2}}\).

   Put it back in its original form (with a radical sign instead of a fractional exponent):  \(-\frac{9}{2}\sqrt{x}\)

\(f'(x) = -\frac{9}{2}\sqrt{x}\)

NEGATIVE FRACTIONAL EXPONENTS

\(f(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt[3]{x}}\)

Negative fractional exponents combine the concepts of negative exponents and fractional exponents.

If \(f(x) = 2x^{-\frac{1}{3}}\), the negative component of the fraction "pushes" the x term to the denominator:    \(\frac{2}{x^{\frac{1}{3}}}\)

The fractional part is just another way of writing the radical sign:    \(\frac{2}{\sqrt[3]{x}}\)

We can easily use the Power Rule to find the derivative of terms with negative fractional exponents.

Rewrite the terms in radical form using negative fractional exponents and then take the derivative in the usual way.

\(f(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt[3]{x}}\)

    Rewrite as a fractional exponent:   \(f(x) = 2x^{-\frac{1}{3}}\)

    Multiply the exponent \(-\frac{1}{3}\) by the coefficient 2:   \((-\frac{1}{3})(2)x^{-\frac{1}{3}}\)

    Subtract 1 from the exponent:   \((-\frac{1}{3})(2)x^{-\frac{4}{3}}\).

    Simplify:   \(-\frac{2}{3}x^{-\frac{4}{3}}\).

    Rewrite it with a positive exponent:  \(-\frac{2}{3x^{\frac{4}{3}}}\)

   Put it back in its original form (with a radical):  \(-\frac{2}{3\sqrt[3]{x}}\)

\(f'(x) = -\frac{2}{3\sqrt[3]{x^4}}\)

NEGATIVE FRACTIONAL EXPONENTS AND NEGATIVE COEFFICIENTS

    Simplify:   \(\frac{6}{3}x^{-\frac{5}{3}}\).

    Reduce the fraction:   \(2x^{-\frac{5}{3}}\).

    Rewrite the x term with a positive exponent:   \(\frac{2}{x^{\frac{5}{3}}}\)

   Put it back in its original form (with a radical sign instead of a fractional exponent):  \(\frac{2}{\sqrt[3]{x^5}}\)

\(f'(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt[3]{x^5}}\)
